Government sounds tough warning to Ferdinand Waititus regime following law limiting jobs to Kiamb

May 16, 2024
Post

- The government has issued a stern warning to Kiambu Governor Ferdinand Waititu's regime after it passed a contentious law

- The government criticized the move by Kiambu County Assembly to source 70 percent of workers from the local community

- Majority Leader and Kiambu Town MCA, Anthony Ikonya, tabled the motion as a measure to protect locals' livelihoods

- Waititu previously lauded MCAs in his county after they passed the controversial law

The national government condemned the move by Kiambu county to pass a law that required companies within its territory to hire 70% of its workforce from the local dominant community.

Government Spokesman Eric Kiraithe said the contentious law was retrogressive because ethnicity was not a qualification for employment anywhere.

"Our statement should not be seen as an attempt to micromanage any county, but we must also point out those things which are retrogressive in terms of greater national goals. County Governments, especially, are public entities and they should be able to recognize even our national diversity and reflect it in their public service outfits,

"The move by Kiambu County Assembly to compel companies to employ 70% of staff from their county is retrogressive. Ethnicity is not a qualification for employment anywhere. Private entities and businesses are encouraged to employ qualified Kenyans with the professional competence to achieve their respective corporate intent," said Kiraithe.

According to prior TUKO.co.ke reports, Majority Leader and Kiambu Town MCA, Anthony Ikonya, tabled the motion citing that locals were unemployed and labour was predominantly outsourced.

The motion, passed on Thursday, December 14, was supported by majority of the MCAs and the Kiambu county governor, Ferdinand Waititu, who lauded the move. Waititu urged other county government to follow in Kiambu's footsteps.

"Our people, whom we represent, should come first since they stood by us and we need to stand by them by making sure they have jobs," said Ndeiya ward MCA Gideon Gachara in support.

The contentious law caused national outrage as the legislators were accused of fanning the flames of secession.

Non-governmental organisations also voiced their concern on the impact of the law, branding it tribal law that did not promote national unity and integration.

NGO Kenya National Chairman Stephen Cheboi demanded the law be reversed despite the legislators possibly having good intentions.

"The Kiambu assembly members might have had good intention in their quest to lobby and advocate for employment to locals but it is prudent that they find alternative ways of approaching the matter rather than making it a law!" said Cheboi.
