Opinion:IEBC Boss Is A Ticking Time Bomb, Act Before Its Too Late

April 15, 2024
Post

After bungling the 2017 General Election and plunging the country into deadly chaos, Independent Electoral and Boundaries Commission (IEBC) chairman Wafula Chebukati is retreading the same treacherous path ahead of 2022 polls.

Despite Chebukati’s assurance that his team had leant from previous mistakes, a series of blunders and glaring irregularities are at play again.

The 2017 General Election

Since the 2017 polls, IEBC has literally been limping and staggering from one mishap to another. From losing election petitions to its composition being declared unconstitutional by two courts, the commission has become the weakest link in Kenya's quest for a free, fair and credible election.

During the presidential petition of 2017, the Supreme Court, which unceremoniously annulled the official results, exposed the rot and incompetence of IEBC commissioners in managing the process.

In a scathing attack that disgraced the commission, the David Maraga-led bench noted that the presidential polls were “neither transparent nor verifiable”.

It emerged from the petition that the commission did not know where its servers were and could not open them for verification after the court-ordered so.

It was clear from the majority verdict that IEBC could not explain why the results from more than 11,000 polling stations were not received at tallying stations as required by law.

The court also noted some ballot papers did not have security features contrary to the dictates of the Constitution.

Further, IEBC was found to have relied on interim reports by election observers to argue their case that the election was credible.

Put simply, the commission did not have evidence of its own to defend its bungled process.

So glaring were the blunders that Maraga noted: “...any court with its right mind would have annulled these results”.

When former vice chair Connie Nkatha Maina and commissioners Margaret Mwachanya and Paul Kurgat resigned in April 2018, they accused Chebukati of ineptitude and inability to lead the commission.

“The institution has continued to be dysfunctional with arbitrary decision making, leaking of internal documents to serve personal goals and pursuing of personal interests. All of which are against the laid down laws that govern the conduct of the commission leadership and staff,” they said in a joint statement.

Former CEO Ezra Chiloba also accused the chairman of incompetence and obsession with making unilateral decisions that often landed the commission in trouble.

However, what is chokingly frustrating is that no reforms have been affected, and the man who recklessly plunged the vehicle into a ditch is still in the driver’s seat.

For the last four years, this visionless, clueless leadership has not been fixed; if anything, it has only been enhanced.

In May 2020, IEBC commissioners displayed unmatched incompetence when they released the 2017 elections report for public consumption.

They were left with eggs on their faces after being alerted that the report was full of glaring yet elementary factual errors. As a result, it was hurriedly pulled down.

Kenyans were perturbed that IEBC, which managed the polls, could not match some MPs with their respective constituencies and political parties. For instance, the commission listed Suba North MP as Noah Odhiambo instead of Millie Odhiambo, who it claimed vied on an ANC ticket instead of ODM.

By his own account, Chebukati has portrayed IEBC as an assembly of incompetent commissioners presiding over a litany of errors.

The commission has also been implicated in myriad procurement scandals. For instance, a memo leaked in 2018 indicated that the commission bought satellite phones worth KSh 848 million but were not used in relaying data from polling stations as intended, forming a basis for nullifying the presidential results.

Chebukati exposed the commission’s high affinity for wastages when presented to parliament a report showing a whopping KSh 202 million had been spent on legal fees alone.

Several law firms also received millions of shillings in suspicious transactions that raised concerns over the chairman's interests in the matter.

While appearing before Parliament, Chiloba, then the CEO, admitted the commission paid one city lawyer, KSh 40 million and another KSh 30 million, for representing a former IEBC chairman in the 2013 presidential petition.

In his defence, Chebukati claimed IEBC hired top-notch lawyers to argue its cases. Well, how many of those cases did the commission win with the so-called top-notch lawyers, some of whom are paid up to KSh40 million per case.?

IEBC losing case

IEBC has lost numerous other cases and plunged the country into a streak of costly by-elections, apart from losing the presidential petition.

About a fortnight ago, the Court of Appeal declared the composition of IEBC illegal, and all the by-elections it presided over unconstitutional.

Despite this wanton misappropriation of public funds, Chebukati continues to endlessly beg for even more funds.

Procurement of IEBC materials is a lucrative money-making enterprise guided by vested interests from top officials down to the clerks.

Therefore, it is not surprising that Kenya runs the most expensive election globally despite having a smaller population than India, the USA, Nigeria, and other countries.

Why should hardworking Kenyan taxpayers continue incurring “extra expenses” courtesy of cartels that blatantly breach procurement laws to enrich themselves?

In his petition to postpone the 2022 polls, former nominated senator Paul Njoroge noted that IEBC made crucial administrative decisions without a substantive CEO in office.

Some of these decisions included setting the election date, procurement of hardware, software, and personnel to run the election.

This could be another avenue for IEBC and its top-notch lawyers to make money.

But there is a more serious matter about Chebukati that must be laid bare. It’s the question of his impartiality as a referee.

Keen observers of the Building Bridges Initiative (BBI) process would tell without batting an eyelid that the much-maligned chair and his team were biased and openly took sides. Was the “Burning Bridges Initiative” tweet a mere typo? And were the exaggerated proposed referendum costs a coincident?

Then came another matter. The commission delivered two versions of the BBI bill to different county assemblies to discredit the process.

A few weeks later, Chebukati appeared before parliament and vehemently opposed the creation of new constituencies to enhance equity, a similar stance held by Deputy President William Ruto!

It’s clear that bias and partisan attributes run deep in Chebukati's veins that he can’t even hide them for optics.

Unfortunately, this is exactly what caused divisions among commissioners in 2017, opening room for the likes of Roselyn Akombe, the so-called Jezebel, to openly take sides.

IEBC cannot conduct 2022 polls with Chebukati as its chair unless we want to gamble again with the country’s well-being.

The failures, blunders and scandals that have become synonymous with his leadership cannot be simply wished away.

We disregarded such signs in 2007, and the country plunged into chaos; we cannot afford a repeat of the same.
