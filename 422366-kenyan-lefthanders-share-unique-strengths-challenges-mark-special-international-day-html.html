August 13 is an International Lefthanders Day to celebrate the uniqueness of left-handed individuals.

The day was first observed in 1976 by Campbell, the founder of Lefthanders International, to raise awareness of the advantages and disadvantages of being left-handed.

Locally, lefthanded Kenyans joined their colleagues in the pre-dominantly righthanded world to commemorate the big day and share some bad and the good.

TUKO.co.ke on Friday, August 13, set out to sample views and challenges some of these people went through while growing before their family members accepted the uniqueness in them.

For Gaudentius Kiiru, as a kindergarten child, she had a problem sharing school desks with her righthanded classmates; she would always push them to the left to create a space for herself.

"Happy to be a left-hander and loving it, it's no joke when we were in school we had a big challenge sharing desks with right-handers, you are always pushing others to the left to accommodate you, but man must live," Kiiru remembered.

Like Kiiru, Waithira Muniu also experienced a bit of a struggle in her early days in school, coming from a family with no known history of a known lefthanded person.

"It's not easy to write while the book is upright, I struggled with that from primary up until no. Any leftie can relate," she adds.

Jane Ogada recounted when her elder sister used to chastise her whenever she used her left hand during meal times, adding that no one should judge God's creation.

While the world has been celebrating this day, there is a need for more awareness on issues faced by left-handers such as the importance of the special needs for left-handed children, and the likelihood for left-handers to develop schizophrenia.

"I am left-handed. My car steering wheel is on the right. My active part of the brain is on the left. When driving You'll realise that I tend to lean so much on the left side," Aswani Moses Zecko said.
